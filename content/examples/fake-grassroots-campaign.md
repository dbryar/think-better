## Pattern

A campaign can be partly real and still manufactured. Real supporters may be recruited into a message architecture funded, scripted, boosted, or strategically framed by people with interests the audience never sees.

That is why "grassroots" is a claim to test, not a label to accept.

## Example: Advance Australia And The "People-Powered" Test

Advance, formerly Advance Australia, is a better Australian case study than a single scandal because it shows the everyday machinery of modern political lobbying: mass email lists, social ads, referendum campaigning, issue branding, professional messaging, and donor opacity.

The point is not that Advance is illegal because it campaigns hard. Campaigning is lawful political participation. The question is whether a campaign that presents itself as people-powered is being materially shaped by a small donor class, large undisclosed donations, or professional party-adjacent staff.

| Signal | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- |
| Many supporters, concentrated money | A campaign can have genuine followers while still depending heavily on a small number of wealthy donors. |
| Unknown funding | Australia's disclosure thresholds can leave voters unable to see who paid for much of the campaign. |
| Professional campaign infrastructure | "Ordinary people are speaking" may be true, but the script, targeting, polling, and ad spend may be professionally produced. |
| Elite anti-elite rhetoric | A campaign can attack "elites" while being funded by wealthy individuals, companies, or investment vehicles. |

Useful source trail:

- The Australian Electoral Commission's Transparency Register is the primary place to inspect political entities, significant third parties, annual returns, and donor records: [AEC Transparency Register](https://www.aec.gov.au/parties_and_representatives/financial_disclosure/transparency-register/).
- The Guardian reported that Advance Australia's 2022-23 donation revenue rose to $5.2 million, with nearly half from unknown sources under disclosure rules: [Rightwing lobby group Advance Australia doubles donation revenue but half of donors unknown](https://www.theguardian.com/australia-news/2024/feb/03/rightwing-lobby-group-advance-australia-doubles-donation-revenue-but-half-of-donors-unknown).
- The Australian Financial Review reported on wealthy backers behind Advance after the Voice referendum campaign: [The heavy hitters behind Australia's biggest conservative lobby group](https://www.afr.com/rich-list/the-heavy-hitters-behind-australia-s-biggest-conservative-lobby-group-20240201-p5f1pt).

## Example: The Tea Party And Sponsored Local Energy

The U.S. Tea Party was not fake in the simple sense. It had real activists, meetings, signs, anger, and local chapters. But credible reporting and scholarship also show that elite conservative networks, including Koch-linked organisations, supplied resources, media amplification, policy framing, and institutional pathways.

That is the useful lesson: astroturfing is often hybrid. The people are real. The infrastructure is not spontaneous.

| Layer | What To Check |
| --- | --- |
| Local anger | What material grievance made people receptive? |
| National messaging | Who supplied repeatable slogans, media hooks, pledge cards, and candidate pipelines? |
| Money | Which donors, foundations, think tanks, or political action groups turned protest energy into electoral machinery? |
| Policy payoff | Did the campaign's policy outcomes match the material interests of its funders? |

Useful source trail:

- The Guardian reported on Koch-linked support for the Tea Party movement: [Tea Party movement: Billionaire Koch brothers who helped it grow](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2010/oct/13/tea-party-billionaire-koch-brothers).
- Cambridge University Press describes the Tea Party as split across grassroots activists, elite conservative groups, and Republican politicians: [The Rise, Fall, and Influence of the Tea Party Insurgency](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/rise-fall-and-influence-of-the-tea-party-insurgency/tea-party/7FC8B7BB5D0C96DCCA6F44672F6BB56D).
- FactCheck.org describes Americans for Prosperity as founded by David Koch and as a major force behind the Tea Party movement: [Americans for Prosperity](https://www.factcheck.org/2014/02/americans-for-prosperity-3/).

## Signals To Check

- Funding is unclear.
- Several posts use identical wording.
- Local photos are stock images or reused across cities.
- Media appearances are arranged before local meetings exist.
- The group avoids naming donors, lobby firms, consultants, or foreign partners.
- The public pitch is identity and freedom; the private ask is regulatory or financial.

## Boring Explanation

The campaign may be coordinated by people with money, ideology, or access at stake. That does not automatically make every supporter fake, but it changes how you read the message.

## Better Questions

Who paid for the website, ads, polling, legal advice, media training, and travel? Which local people are actually represented? What would the campaign have to disclose before you called it grassroots?
