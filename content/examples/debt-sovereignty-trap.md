## Pattern

"Take back control" is emotionally powerful because sovereignty matters. The trap is when leaders sell sovereignty as freedom from constraint while hiding the constraints they are creating: foreign-currency debt, weak exports, inflation, capital flight, import dependence, patronage spending, and creditors with leverage.

| Promise | Risk To Check |
| --- | --- |
| "We will ignore the technocrats." | Are the numbers wrong, or merely inconvenient? |
| "We will borrow for the people." | Borrow in which currency, at what interest rate, and with what repayment plan? |
| "We will protect local industry." | Is protection building capacity, or just hiding inefficiency and raising costs? |
| "Foreign institutions are the enemy." | Is the country gaining bargaining power, or losing access to finance because trust collapsed? |

## Argentina: Repeated Defaults And Policy Whiplash

Argentina is useful because it resists simple left-right storytelling. Different governments have mixed populism, austerity, protectionism, liberalisation, debt, currency controls, and emergency lending. The warning is not "one ideology caused everything." The warning is that repeated credibility failures eventually let creditors, inflation, and exchange rates discipline a country more harshly than voters expected.

Useful source trail:

- Council on Foreign Relations notes Argentina's repeated sovereign defaults and reliance on external funding: [Argentina's Struggle for Stability](https://www.cfr.org/backgrounders/argentinas-struggle-stability).
- The IMF's lessons volume describes the 2001-02 default as one of Argentina's worst crises: [Lessons from the Crisis in Argentina](https://www.elibrary.imf.org/downloadpdf/display/book/9781589063594/9781589063594.pdf).
- WTO review material described Argentina's trade-policy complexity and the costs of short-term trade interventions: [WTO Trade Policy Review: Argentina](https://www.wto.org/english/tratop_e/tpr_e/s277_sum_e.pdf).

## Sri Lanka: Tax Cuts, Debt, Imports, And Collapse

Sri Lanka shows how policy choices, debt exposure, external shocks, and lost confidence can combine. A sovereignty story can collapse quickly when a country cannot finance imports, fuel, medicine, or food.

Useful source trail:

- The World Bank summarised Sri Lanka's crisis and recovery constraints: [Sri Lanka Development Update](https://www.worldbank.org/en/country/srilanka/publication/sri-lanka-development-update).
- The IMF described Sri Lanka's reform programme after the 2022 crisis: [Sri Lanka's Economic Reform Program is Starting to Work](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2024/03/01/022224oped-srilanka-economic-reform-program-is-starting-to-work).

## Ghana And The Broader Debt Warning

Debt trouble is not just a dramatic collapse story. It can be a slow narrowing of public choices, where interest payments crowd out health, education, infrastructure, and climate resilience.

Useful source trail:

- The World Bank's Global Waves of Debt project warns that the first three modern debt waves ended in crises across many emerging and developing economies: [Global Waves of Debt](https://www.worldbank.org/en/research/publication/waves-of-debt).
- The World Economic Forum summarises how rising sovereign debt squeezes developing-country budgets and public investment: [Global debt levels are on the rise](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2024/09/global-debt-crisis-development-chief-economy/).
- The IMF's public financial management blog warns emerging markets face rising debt-service pressure: [Emerging Markets Face a Perfect Storm](https://blog-pfm.imf.org/en/pfmblog/2025/04/emerging-markets-face-a-perfect-storm).

## Better Habit

Do not ask only whether a policy sounds patriotic. Ask whether it increases the country's future room to move.
