# Editorial Style

This project teaches questions, not loyalty. It should feel like a public-interest field guide: calm, sharp, practical, inspectable, and correctable.

## Core Positioning

> This project does not tell you what to believe. It teaches you how to check claims, incentives, evidence, and manipulation, including our own.

## Good Scepticism

Good scepticism asks:

- What is the claim?
- What is the evidence?
- Who benefits?
- What would disprove it?
- What is the boring explanation?

## Bad Scepticism

Bad scepticism says:

> The official story is false, therefore my preferred alternative is true.

The project should help readers avoid that shortcut. Replacing one unaccountable authority with another is not critical thinking.

## Tone Guardrails

- No "wake up sheeple" energy.
- No secret-knowledge framing.
- No forbidden-book mystique.
- No anarchist-handbook aesthetic.
- No guru or chosen-few language.
- No totalising explanations.
- No calls for violence, harassment, sabotage, illegality, or dehumanisation.
- No party-political endorsements in core material.
- No "all politicians/media/experts are corrupt" blanket claims.
- Prefer calibrated trust over blanket distrust.
- Prefer "here are better questions" over "we know the truth."
- Avoid recruitment dynamics common to cultic, conspiratorial, extremist, or manipulative movements.
- Keep everything public, inspectable, correctable, and forkable.

## Design Guardrails

- Plain typography.
- High readability.
- Good mobile layout.
- Good print layout.
- No edgy hacker aesthetic.
- No revolutionary red/black cliche.
- No cultish symbolism.
- Calm, practical, resilient presentation.

## Accessibility Guardrails

- Use semantic HTML.
- Keep contrast strong.
- Keep navigation keyboard-accessible.
- Make pages print-friendly.
- Do not rely on colour alone.
- Prefer simple language.
